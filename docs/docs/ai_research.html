A Research Agenda for the Economics of Transformative AI

Authors: Erik Brynjolfsson, Anton Korinek, Ajay K. Agrawal

Publication: NBER Working Paper No. 34256, September 2025

As AI capabilities advance rapidly, our economic institutions struggle to keep pace. This research agenda identifies nine Grand Challenges to help navigate AI's transformative economic implications.

Why This Matters Now

  • Accelerating Timelines: Median prediction for general AI moved from 2062 (in 2020) to 2033 today
  • Current Impact: Generative AI already affects 19% of US workers significantly
  • Future Potential: Productivity growth rates could double in coming years
  • The Gap: Technology evolves faster than institutions, creating both risks and opportunities

"Organizations and processes evolve far more slowly than technology itself—which makes our work all the more urgent."

What is Transformative AI?

Economic Definition

AI that enables a sustained increase in total factor productivity growth of at least 3-5x historical averages

Example: "Powerful AI"

"A country of geniuses in a datacenter" - Dario Amodei

  • Intelligence surpassing most capable humans across multiple fields
  • Autonomous complex task performance
  • Still constrained by physical bottlenecks and computational limits

Nine Grand Challenges

1. Economic Growth

How will TAI change growth rates and bottlenecks?

2. Innovation

Which processes will TAI transform first?

3. Income Distribution

Will TAI reduce or increase inequality?

4. Decision-Making Power

How will economic and political power concentrate?

5. Geoeconomics

How will TAI reshape international relations?

6. Information Flows

Truth vs. misinformation in AI-mediated world

7. AI Safety

Balancing benefits with catastrophic risks

8. Human Well-being

Meaning and purpose in post-work society

9. Transition Dynamics

Managing the shift to TAI economy

Challenge 1: Economic Growth

Key Questions:

  • How can we detect early signs of an AI-driven "growth explosion"?
  • What new bottlenecks will emerge? (Energy, compute, raw materials)
  • How will human capital's role evolve?
  • Which skills remain valuable as AI capabilities grow?

Current Constraints: Limited workers, human capital, physical capital

Future Constraints: Energy availability, computational resources, ecological limits

Challenge 2: Innovation & Discovery

Transformative Potential:

  • Automation of Science: Reduced costs and time for experimentation
  • Cross-disciplinary Innovation: Breaking down traditional academic silos
  • Democratized Innovation: Small firms accessing sophisticated R&D capabilities
  • Global vs. Local Discoveries: Moving beyond disciplinary constraints

"Many transformative innovations emerge from integrating knowledge across distinct fields, yet human cognitive limitations often prevent experts from identifying novel connections."

Challenge 3: Income Distribution

Labor Market Implications:

  • Job Displacement vs. Creation: Will TAI displace more jobs than it creates?
  • Wage Effects: If machines substitute for workers, wages may fall to machine costs
  • Remaining Jobs: Focus on human-centered, intrinsic value work
  • Social Safety Nets: Current systems designed around labor income

Key Concern: If TAI purveyors capture most gains, will this increase inequality or will a growing economy "lift all boats"?

Challenges 4 & 5: Power & Geoeconomics

Economic Concentration:

  • Will TAI be dominated by few systems or many?
  • How will TAI affect competition in other industries?
  • Could centrally-planned economies gain advantages?

Global Implications:

  • Military Applications: Impact on global security balance
  • Economic Power Shifts: Uneven productivity gains across nations
  • Regulatory Challenges: Preventing regulatory arbitrage between countries

Challenges 6 & 7: Information & Safety

Information Quality:

  • How will AI-mediated systems affect truth vs. misinformation?
  • Can AI content overwhelm human-produced information?
  • Impact on democratic governance and social cohesion

AI Safety & Alignment:

  • Cost-Benefit Analysis: Safety investments vs. economic benefits
  • Race Dynamics: Pressure to prioritize speed over safety
  • Existential Risks: When to slow or halt AI progress?

Challenges 8 & 9: Well-being & Transitions

Human Meaning:

  • How do people find purpose if "the economic problem is solved"?
  • Difference between voluntary retirement vs. involuntary unemployment
  • Designing policies for post-work fulfillment

Managing Transitions:

  • Speed Mismatch: Fast AI development vs. slow institutional change
  • Complementary Factors: Skills, regulations, organizations adapting slowly
  • Crisis Prevention: Avoiding mass unemployment and system failures

Research Methodologies

Theoretical Approaches

Growth models, micro-to-macro frameworks, welfare theorems for unprecedented scenarios

TAI Dashboard

Economic indicators tracking substitution of labor for capital, productivity measures

New Welfare Metrics

Measuring value of zero-marginal-cost goods, massive online choice experiments

Task-Level Analysis

Fine-grained analysis using job postings and résumés rather than broad occupational categories

AI Agent Simulations

LLM-based agents simulating economic behavior at scale

Scenario Planning

Structured approach for multiple plausible futures given high uncertainty

Economic Indicators for TAI

What to Watch:

  • Factor Inputs: Compute, energy, labor devoted to AI development
  • Technological: AI capabilities, robot performance, labor substitutability
  • Production/Output: Total factor productivity, labor demand changes
  • Financial Markets: AI company valuations, VC investments, energy prices
  • Industry-Level: New AI-enabled industries, market concentration changes
  • Distribution: Labor vs. capital share, Gini coefficient trends
  • International: Global trade patterns, cross-country GDP gaps

Policy Domains Affected

  • Labor Laws: Adapting to TAI-driven employment changes
  • Taxation Systems: Distributing TAI-generated wealth
  • Education: Preparing workers for AI-augmented economy
  • Social Insurance: New income distribution mechanisms
  • Antitrust: Managing TAI-driven market concentration
  • Intellectual Property: Innovation incentives in AI age
  • Environmental Policy: Managing TAI's ecological footprint
  • International Coordination: Global governance of TAI development

The Path Forward

Two Scenarios:

Positive: Vastly enhanced wealth, improved welfare, fairly distributed benefits

Negative: Increased inequality, mass unemployment, social instability, catastrophic risks

Urgent Need:

  • Proactive research agenda before TAI arrives
  • Interdisciplinary collaboration (economics, computer science, policy)
  • More investment in understanding economic implications
  • Building institutional capacity for research and policy response

Goal: Navigate toward broadly shared prosperity and human flourishing as transformative AI unfolds
